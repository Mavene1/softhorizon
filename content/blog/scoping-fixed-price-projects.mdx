Every delivery problem we've had on a fixed-price engagement traces back to the same root cause: something was ambiguous at scoping time, and both sides read the ambiguity in their own favor.

That's not a client problem or an agency problem — it's what happens when scope gets written down before anyone has really pressure-tested it.

## The three questions that catch the most risk

We now run every fixed-price scope through three questions before it goes into a statement of work:

1. **What does "done" look like, specifically?** Not "a working checkout flow" — which payment providers, which currencies, what happens on a failed payment.
2. **What's explicitly out of scope?** Writing down what we're *not* building is often more useful than the list of what we are.
3. **Who owns the decision if requirements change mid-build?** Getting this agreed up front turns a scope disagreement into a five-minute conversation instead of a two-week stall.

## Why this matters more than it seems

None of this is about protecting margin at the client's expense — the projects where scoping went well are also the ones with the best client relationships afterward. Ambiguity is what erodes trust, not honest conversations about tradeoffs.

> A scope document that takes an extra week to get right is almost always cheaper than the change-request conversation you'll have in month two without it.

## Where we still get it wrong

Integrations with client-owned systems we don't have visibility into during scoping remain the hardest category to estimate accurately. Our current approach is to explicitly flag those as a discovery spike rather than guessing — it costs a small amount of time up front, but it's saved us from underscoping more than once.
