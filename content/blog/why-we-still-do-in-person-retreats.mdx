We're not a fully remote company, but most of the working year still looks like it: engineers embedded at client sites, designers working from home two or three days a week, standups over video call more often than not.

So when we budget for a week-long, all-team retreat every year, it's a fair question — is that actually worth it, or is it just a nice-to-have that survives budget cuts because nobody wants to be the one who cancels it?

## What a video call can't replicate

The honest answer is that most of the value doesn't come from the planned sessions — it comes from what happens around them. The conversation over lunch between an engineer and a designer who've worked on the same project for six months but never met in person. The junior hire who finally feels comfortable asking a "dumb" question after three days of informal context.

We've tried to name what specifically doesn't transfer to remote work:

- Trust that forms faster face-to-face than over a dozen video calls
- Cross-team context that normally only spreads through overheard conversations
- The kind of honest, low-stakes feedback that's easier to give in person

## What we actually do with the week

Less structured time than you'd expect. Roughly:

1. One full day of retrospective — what worked across the year's projects, what didn't
2. A day of cross-team pairing, deliberately mixing people who don't normally work together
3. The rest is unstructured — and that's on purpose

> The sessions we plan are rarely what people remember a year later. It's almost always something that happened in the gaps.

## Is it worth the cost?

For a team our size, yes — measurably so. Voluntary turnover among people who've been to at least one retreat is lower than among newer hires who haven't yet, though we're cautious about reading too much into a small sample. What we're confident about is softer: the team simply collaborates better across engagements for a few months after each one.
