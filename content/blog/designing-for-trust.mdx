When you design a SaaS product, you can assume a baseline of digital literacy and a user who chose to be there. Neither assumption holds for a citizen filing a permit application on a government portal — they might be doing it for the first time, under time pressure, possibly on a shared or low-end device.

That changes almost everything about how we approach the work.

## Clarity beats cleverness, every time

On most of our product work, we look for opportunities to delight — a well-placed animation, a clever empty state. On a government portal, every one of those instincts got tested against a simpler question: *does this make the next step obvious?*

If it didn't, it got cut. That meant:

- Fewer, larger touch targets — no dense toolbars
- Plain-language labels instead of internal department terminology
- Progress indicators on every multi-step form, always visible, never assumed

## Trust is a design problem, not just a security problem

Citizens don't read privacy policies before deciding whether a government site feels legitimate. They read visual signals — is the page well-made, does it look official, does it behave the way official things behave.

We leaned on:

- Consistent, predictable navigation with no surprises
- Visible confirmation at every step that data was received (a submitted form should never leave you wondering if it worked)
- A support contact that's easy to find, not buried three clicks deep

> The biggest compliment we got during user testing wasn't about a feature — it was a participant saying "this feels like it's actually going to work," before they'd submitted anything at all.

## What we'd tell a team starting this kind of project

Budget real time for in-person user testing with people outside your usual user research pool — ideally people who don't consider themselves particularly comfortable with technology. What reads as "obvious" to a design team is rarely obvious to the person who most needs the service to just work.
